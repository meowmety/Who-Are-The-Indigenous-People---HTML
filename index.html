Learning Packet 01: Who are Indigenous Peoples?

I. Introduction

Indigenous Peoples comprise approximately six percent of the world's population. Despite being a minority, they play a crucial role in supporting environmental sustainability, actively contributing to the preservation of 80% of the world's remaining biodiversity. They are the keepers of wisdom and skills regarding adaptation to the natural environment.

Lesson Objectives:
  • Define Indigenous Peoples by comparing different perspectives.
  • Recognize the contributions made by Indigenous peoples to society and community.
  • Create a conceptual framework showing the variables of being "Indigenous".

II. Global Realities and Challenges

There are approximately 476 million Indigenous Peoples living in more than 90 countries worldwide, representing over 5,000 distinct groups. Seventy percent of this population resides in Asia.

Common challenges include:

  • Forced removal from ancestral lands.
  • Restrictions on practicing cultural traditions.
  • Discrimination in legal systems and susceptibility to violence.
  • Accusations of treason or terrorism for protecting resource-rich territories.

III. Core Definitions

Indigenous Originating or occurring naturally in a particular place.
Indigenous Peoples Distinct social and cultural groups with collective ancestral ties to the lands and natural resources where they live or from which they have been displaced.
Self-determination Refers to each person's ability to make choices and manage their own life.
Self-identification The act of identifying yourself as a particular kind of person.

IV. Perspectives on Identity

Academic Definitions

  • Franke Wilmer: Emphasizes tradition-based cultures and political autonomy prior to colonization.
  • S. James Anaya: Defines them as living descendants of pre-invasion inhabitants whose roots are deeply embedded in their lands.
  • Fred Riggs: Focuses on four elements: cultural level, historical sequence, political position (marginalization), and geographic area.
  • Benedict Kingsbury: Suggests a "constructivist approach" focusing on self-identification, historical experience of disruption, and a wish to retain distinct identity.

Organizational Definitions

  • World Bank: Identifies groups based on close attachment to ancestral territories and the use of an indigenous language different from the national one.
  • ILO Convention No. 169: Emphasizes self-identification and descent from populations present during conquest or colonization.
  • IWGIA: Focuses on disadvantaged descendants of those who inhabited a territory prior to state formation.

V. Indigenous Peoples in the Philippines

Indigenous Peoples in the Philippines have preserved a substantial portion of their pre-colonial culture and social organization. They comprise an estimated 10 to 20 percent of the national population.

Northern Philippines

This region includes various Igorot tribes such as the Bontoc, Ibaloi, Ifugao, Isneg, Kalinga, Kankana-ey, and Tinguian. It also includes the Negritos, who formerly dominated the highlands across the islands.

Southern Philippines

Mindanao groups are collectively called the Lumad, including the Manobo, Bukidnon, Bagobo, Mandaya, Mansaka, and Tagakaulo. Other groups include the Subanon (Zamboanga), B'laan, Teduray, T'boli (Cotabato), and the Sama/Sinama tribes of Tawi-Tawi.
